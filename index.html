Decriminalizing Prostitution

A free society is built on the principle that individuals should be allowed to make decisions about their own lives, especially when those decisions involve voluntary exchanges between consenting adults. Prostitution falls squarely within this category. When two adults agree to exchange money for a service, there is no inherent violation of rights. Criminalizing such an arrangement imposes external moral judgments on private choices.

In South Africa, where economic inequality and unemployment remain serious challenges, many individuals turn to prostitution as a means of survival or financial independence. This choice may not align with traditional social values, but it is often a rational response to difficult circumstances. Denying people the ability to make this choice does not eliminate the underlying conditions—it simply removes one of the few options available to them.

The current legal framework forces prostitution into the shadows. As a result, those involved operate without protection, making them more vulnerable to violence, exploitation, and abuse. They are less likely to report crimes, less able to negotiate fair terms, and more exposed to dangerous environments. The law, rather than protecting them, increases their risk.

Decriminalization would reverse this dynamic. It would allow individuals to operate openly, access legal protection, and improve their working conditions. It would also reduce the influence of criminal networks that thrive in illegal markets. Instead of punishing people for their choices, the state would recognize their autonomy and provide a safer framework within which they can act.

At its core, the issue is one of control over one’s own body and labor. If a person chooses to offer a service and another chooses to pay for it, the state has little justification to intervene. Respecting this principle leads to a more consistent, rational, and humane legal system.

Legalizing Kidney Sales

The right to make decisions about one’s own body is one of the most fundamental aspects of personal freedom. If individuals are not allowed to determine how their own bodies are used, then their autonomy is severely limited. This principle becomes especially relevant when considering whether people should be allowed to sell a kidney.

A kidney is part of an individual’s body, and the decision to part with it—whether for medical, personal, or financial reasons—should ultimately rest with that individual. Preventing someone from selling a kidney assumes that the state has greater authority over a person’s body than the person themselves. This is a serious intrusion into personal liberty.

Many individuals who consider selling a kidney do so as a calculated decision. They may be facing financial hardship or seeking a way to improve their circumstances. If they understand the risks and still choose to proceed, it reflects a deliberate judgment that the benefits outweigh the costs. Denying them this option does not protect them; it simply limits their ability to act on their own priorities.

Prohibition has also led to the growth of illegal organ markets, where transactions occur without proper medical oversight or legal safeguards. This environment increases the likelihood of exploitation and harm. By contrast, a legal and regulated system would ensure informed consent, fair compensation, and safe medical procedures.

Such a system could also benefit those in need of transplants. There is a global shortage of organs, and many patients die while waiting for a donor. Allowing individuals to sell kidneys could increase supply, saving lives while also providing financial opportunities for sellers. Both parties stand to gain, making the exchange mutually beneficial.

Arguments that this practice would exploit the poor often overlook a key point: restricting choice does not eliminate inequality. It merely removes one possible way for individuals to improve their situation. A more consistent approach is to respect people’s capacity to make decisions for themselves, even when those decisions involve risk.

Legalizing Weed Sales

The ability to engage in voluntary exchange is a cornerstone of a functioning society. When individuals choose to produce, sell, or consume a product without harming others, there is little justification for preventing them from doing so. The sale of cannabis is a clear example of such an activity.

In South Africa, private use of cannabis has already been recognized as a matter of personal choice. However, prohibiting its sale creates a contradiction. Individuals are allowed to consume cannabis, but not to participate in a legal market that supplies it. This inconsistency pushes the trade into informal or illegal channels.

When a product is in demand, banning its sale does not eliminate that demand. Instead, it creates opportunities for unregulated markets to emerge. These markets often operate without oversight, increasing the likelihood of criminal activity, unsafe products, and exploitation. The current approach, therefore, does not solve the problem—it shifts it into a less controlled and more dangerous space.

Legalizing the sale of weed would bring the industry into the open. It would allow for regulation, quality control, and taxation. Entrepreneurs would have the opportunity to build legitimate businesses, create jobs, and contribute to the economy. Consumers would have access to safer, regulated products.

Beyond the economic benefits, legalization respects the principle that individuals should be able to make their own choices about what they consume. While cannabis may carry risks, those risks are not unique. Many legal substances, such as alcohol and tobacco, also pose health concerns, yet they are regulated rather than prohibited.

A consistent legal framework should focus on minimizing harm while preserving freedom. Allowing the sale of cannabis achieves both goals. It reduces the influence of illegal markets and recognizes the right of individuals to make decisions about their own lives.
