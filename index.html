Essay 1: Decriminalizing Prostitution

From an ethical egoist perspective, individuals should be free to pursue actions that maximize their self-interest, provided they do not violate the rights of others. Prostitution, when conducted voluntarily between consenting adults, represents a mutual exchange of value. Criminalizing it only forces individuals into dangerous underground markets, increasing risk and reducing their ability to protect themselves.

In South Africa, where economic inequality is high, prostitution can serve as a rational choice for individuals seeking financial independence. Ethical egoism supports this autonomy. By decriminalizing prostitution, the state would allow individuals to operate openly, improve safety conditions, and make rational decisions about their own bodies and labor.

Essay 2: Legalizing the Sale of Kidneys

Ethical egoism asserts that individuals own their bodies and therefore have the right to decide how to use them. Preventing someone from selling a kidney is a direct violation of bodily autonomy. If an individual rationally determines that selling a kidney benefits their life—financially or otherwise—they should be legally allowed to do so.

A regulated market for organ sales could reduce black market activity, increase supply for life-saving transplants, and provide individuals with new economic opportunities. Ethical egoism does not demand sacrifice; it permits mutually beneficial exchanges where both parties gain value.

Essay 3: Legalizing the Sale of Weed

From the standpoint of ethical egoism, prohibiting the sale of cannabis restricts individual freedom without sufficient justification. If an individual chooses to grow, sell, or consume weed, and others willingly engage in that transaction, there is no moral basis for state interference.

Legalizing the sale of weed in South Africa would empower entrepreneurs, reduce criminal activity, and allow individuals to pursue economic gain in a regulated environment. Ethical egoism supports such freedom, emphasizing personal choice, voluntary exchange, and rational self-interest.
