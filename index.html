Discussions on South African Politics & Philosophy

Exploring the boundaries of personal liberty, bodily autonomy, and legislative reform in the modern South African state.

The Case for Decriminalizing Sex Work

In South Africa, the current legal framework surrounding sex work remains a vestige of colonial and apartheid-era morality laws. This essay argues that decriminalization is not merely a social preference, but a human rights necessity.

By moving toward a model similar to New Zealand’s, we can ensure that sex workers have access to COIDA (Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act) and general labor protections, effectively removing the "shadow economy" status that leads to police extortion and violence...

Commercializing Cannabis: Beyond Private Use

While the Constitutional Court made strides in the 2018 Prince judgment regarding private cultivation, the "trade" remains largely illegal. This creates a logical fallacy: one has the right to consume, but no safe, regulated legal framework to acquire.

From an economic standpoint, South Africa's climate is ideal for "Dagga" cultivation. Legalizing the sale would empower small-scale rural farmers and provide a significant tax revenue stream, shifting the industry from the hands of cartels to the formal economy...

Organ Autonomy: The Ethics of Kidney Sales

Perhaps the most controversial frontier of bodily autonomy is the right to alienate one's own organs for compensation. Current South African law prohibits the sale of human tissue, yet we face a chronic shortage of donors.

Philosophically, if an individual owns their body, they should have the right to enter into a contract for the sale of a kidney under strict medical supervision. This essay explores how a regulated market could eliminate the dangerous "black market" while saving thousands of lives currently lost to renal failure...
