Structural Arguments on South African Liberty

1. Decriminalization of Adult Consensual Sex Work

The prohibition of sex work in South Africa is an inconsistent application of the right to bodily integrity. To maintain a logically sound legal system, the following must be considered:

  • Premise A: The South African Constitution guarantees the right to freedom and security of the person, including the right to make decisions concerning reproduction and the body.
  • Premise B: Criminalization removes legal protections from workers, increasing the risk of violence and exploitation without reducing the prevalence of the work.
  • Conclusion: Decriminalizing sex work is the only policy that aligns with constitutional mandates while simultaneously improving public safety.

2. Legalization of Cannabis Commerce

The current legal "limbo" regarding the sale of cannabis creates a market vacuum that benefits unregulated actors rather than the citizens.

  • Premise A: Private use and cultivation are already legally recognized rights in South Africa.
  • Premise B: Preventing the regulated sale of a legal substance forces citizens to interact with illicit markets to exercise their rights.
  • Conclusion: Establishing a transparent, regulated commercial framework is a logical necessity to uphold the spirit of the court's recent rulings.

3. Regulated Markets for Organ Donation

The ethical objection to the sale of organs often fails to account for the direct cost in human lives caused by current shortages.

  • Premise A: A person has a higher degree of ownership over their internal organs than any other property; thus, the right to alienate (sell) that property should be protected.
  • Premise B: A state-governed market would provide a safe, clinical environment for donors who are currently driven to dangerous, unregulated international black markets.
  • Conclusion: Transitioning to a regulated compensation model for kidney donation respects individual autonomy and solves the chronic supply shortage.
